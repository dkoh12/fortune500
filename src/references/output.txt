<h3 style="text-align: center;"><strong>Index Methodology</strong></h3>
<p>The S&amp;P 500 is most well-known for containing the 500 largest companies in the United States when measured by market capitalization.</p>
<p>The Index also has other criteria, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Universe: </strong>All constituents must be U.S. companies.</li>
<li><strong>Eligibility Market Cap:</strong> Companies with a market capitalization of US$6.1 billion or greater.</li>
<li><strong>Public Float:</strong> At least 50% of shares outstanding must be available for trading.</li>
<li><strong>Financial Viability:</strong> Companies must have positive as-reported earnings over the most recent quarter, as well as over the most recent four quarters (summed together).</li>
<li><strong>Adequate Liquidity and Reasonable Price:</strong> Consists of highly tradable common stocks, with active and deep markets.</li>
</ul>

<h3 style="text-align: center;"><strong>The Historical Performance of the S&amp;P 500</strong></h3>
<p>The S&amp;P 500&#8217;s historical performance over the long-term is often cited as anywhere between 7% and 10% on an annualized basis. The S&amp;P 500 has generated total inflation-adjusted returns (including reinvested dividends) of 6.9% from January of 1871 through September 2019.</p>
<p>Source: <a href="https://dqydj.com/sp-500-return-calculator/">DQYDJ</a></p>
<p>And, the S&amp;P 500&#8217;s Sharpe ratio has averaged around 0.33 over the long run. You can see the <a href="https://www.suredividend.com/sharpe-ratio-stocks/">Sharpe ratios of individual S&amp;P 500 components here</a>.</p>
<p>Interestingly, the 4 worst total return days for The S&amp;P 500 since 1990 all occurred in 2008:</p>
<ol>
<li>October 15th, 2008 | 9.0% decline</li>
<li>December 1st, 2008 | 8.9% decline</li>
<li>September 29th, 2008 | 8.8% decline</li>
<li>October 9th, 2008 | 7.6% decline</li>
</ol>
<p>The 4 best total return days for The S&amp;P 500 since 1990 are shown below as well for balance:</p>
<ol>
<li>October 13th, 2008 | 11.6% increase</li>
<li>October 28th, 2008 | 10.8% increase</li>
<li>March 23rd, 2009 | 7.1% increase</li>
<li>November 13th, 2008 | 6.9% increase</li>
</ol>
<p>The data above shows just <em>how volatile</em> the market was during the last half of 2008 and the first quarter of 2009. The Great Recession was an especially chaotic market period.</p>
<p>The S&amp;P 500 generated positive performance in September 2019. During the month, the S&amp;P 500 ETF (SPY) generated total returns of 1.9%. This performance comes after a 1.7% decline in August.</p>
<p>For 2019 through September, the S&amp;P 500 ETF has generated total returns of 20.4%. This is already well ahead of average annual returns for the S&amp;P 500, and we are only 3/4 of the way through the year. We will update this article monthly to continue tracking the S&amp;P 500&#8217;s performance.</p>
<p><a name="compositioncharacteristics"></a></p>
<h3 style="text-align: center;"><strong>Index Composition &amp; Characteristics</strong></h3>
<p>Understanding the characteristics of the S&amp;P 500 is useful because it allows investors to know the traits of the &#8220;average&#8221; large capitalization company in the United States. According to the index provider (<a href="https://ca.spindices.com/indices/equity/sp-500">Standard &amp; Poor&#8217;s</a>), the S&amp;P 500 currently has the following characteristics:</p>
<ul>
<li>Number of constituents: 505</li>
<li>Mean total market cap: $51.3 billion</li>
<li>Largest total market cap: $1,061.5 billion [<a href="https://www.suredividend.com/growth-of-income/">Microsoft (MSFT)</a>]</li>
<li>Smallest total market cap: $2.8 billion</li>
<li>Median total market cap: $22.4 billion</li>
<li>Weight of top 10 constituents: 21.6%</li>
</ul>

<p><a name="benchmark"></a></p>
<h3 style="text-align: center;"><strong>How To Use The S&amp;P 500 As A Benchmark</strong></h3>
<p>Investors can gain passive access to a broad basket of S&amp;P 500 stocks by investing in index ETFs that track the benchmark. Accordingly, it is useful to ensure that your portfolio&#8217;s performance is comparable to the S&amp;P 500, especially on a risk-adjusted basis.</p>
<p>The easiest way to measure the performance of the S&amp;P 500 Index is by using a free financial tool like <a href="http://www.google.com/finance">Google Finance</a> or <a href="http://www.yahoo.com/finance">Yahoo! Finance</a>. Download historical data of S&amp;P 500 Index levels from these resources, and compare its performance to the performance of your portfolio. If it turns out that the index is trouncing your performance over long periods of time, then changing to a passive investment strategy might be a wise decision.</p>
